Derivative of Sine

We shall prove the formula for the derivative of the sine function by using the first principle. Let us suppose that the function is of the form \(y = \sin x\). First, we take the increment or small change in the function: \[ \Delta y = \sin (x + \Delta x) - \sin (x) \] Since, \[ \sin(A+B) = \sin(A)\cos(B) + \cos(A)\sin(B) \] we get: \[ \Delta y = \sin(x)\cos(\Delta x) + \cos(x)\sin(\Delta x) - \sin(x) \] Dividing both sides by \(\Delta x \), we get: \[ \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{\sin(x)(\cos(\Delta x) - 1) + \cos(x)\sin(\Delta x)}{\Delta x} \] \[ \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \sin(x)\frac{(\cos(\Delta x) - 1)}{\Delta x} + \cos(x)\frac{\sin(\Delta x)}{\Delta x} \] Taking the limit of both sides as \( \Delta x \to 0 \), we have \[ \lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \sin(x)\lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{(\cos(\Delta x) - 1)}{\Delta x} + \cos(x)\lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{\sin(\Delta x)}{\Delta x} \] Given that \(\quad \lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{(\cos(\Delta x) - 1)}{\Delta x} = 0 \quad \) and \(\quad \lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{\sin(\Delta x)}{\Delta x} = 1\), \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \sin(x)(0) + \cos(x)(1) \] \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \cos(x)(1) \]
